Computation and

Information Processing
Faculty associated with the computation and information processing emphasis of our program are studying questions that span the range of human perceptual and cognitive abilities, from low-level visual perception to aspects of consciousness. The common thread in these inquiries is a commitment to understanding the sources of information relevant to a particular cognitive phenomenon, along with the internal processes that operate on those sources of information. This commitment entails addressing theoretical issues that range from fine-grained aspects of information processing to large-scale questions about cognitive architecture.

Faculty draw on a range of theoretical and experimental approaches in pursuing these questions. On the theoretical side, students can expect to be exposed to the use of formal logic, general mathematical representations, and computational models, including computational representations of neural circuits and cognitive processes implemented in cognitive architectures. On the experimental side, students have
opportunities to gain experience with a range of precise methods of stimulus presentation and response measurement, including tachistoscopic displays, reaction time methods, response force measurement, eye-tracking, and electrophysiology.

Faculty and grad students meet for a weekly brown-bag speakers' series, involving everyone from the cognitive area. In addition, there are topical speakers' series focused on perception and action and on cognitive neuroscience.
